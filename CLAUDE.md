BaryGraph
A CM-Topology Knowledge Graph Architecture
Version 1.0  ·  April 2026

BaryGraph is a knowledge graph architecture in which every relationship is a first-class object — a BaryEdge — that sits between two nodes exactly as a barycenter sits between two centers of mass. The graph is organized into a strict hierarchy of levels (1–15) mirroring the CM Theory framework. At higher levels, BaryEdges themselves become nodes, producing MetaBary connections. When combined with vector embeddings stored in MongoDB Atlas, the architecture enables cross-domain retrieval that standard nearest-neighbor search cannot produce: queries return not only similar nodes but the relationship structures connecting them, climbing the hierarchy to surface bridges invisible within any single domain.

1. Motivation
Standard knowledge graphs treat relationships as thin directed edges: label + weight between two nodes. Standard vector databases treat documents as points in embedding space, with retrieval by cosine similarity. Both approaches lose relational structure that is often the most valuable part of a corpus.
Consider two papers: one reporting an unexplained spacecraft flyby anomaly in planetary science, another reporting an anomalous orbital residual in stellar dynamics. Neither cites the other. Their vectors are distant in embedding space. Standard retrieval returns neither when querying the other. Yet they describe the same underlying physical phenomenon at different scales.
BaryGraph is designed to make that connection first-class and queryable. The relationship between the two papers is not a thin edge — it is a document with its own vector, its own properties, its own position in the hierarchy. It can be retrieved directly, traversed upward, and used to discover further bridges.
2. Core Concepts
2.1  The Triad as Atomic Unit
Every connection in BaryGraph is a triad of three entities:
CM₁  →  BaryEdge  →  CM₂
CM₁ and CM₂ are knowledge nodes (papers, concepts, domains, authors). The BaryEdge is the relationship document sitting between them. It is not a pointer — it is a first-class stored object with its own embedding vector, its own properties, and its own identity. It can be retrieved, queried, and promoted to a CM role at higher hierarchy levels.

A BaryEdge always connects two nodes at the same level. Cross-level connections are handled exclusively by HierarchyLinks (Section 3.3).
2.3  The BaryEdge Vector
Every BaryEdge has an embedding vector computed as a weighted mixture of its two CM vectors plus a relationship-type vector:
bary_vec = normalize( q·v(CM₁) + q·v(CM₂) + (1−q)·v(type) )
where q is connection quality (0–1) and v(type) is the embedding of the edge type label. A strong connection (q → 1) produces a barycenter close to both CM vectors simultaneously. A weak connection (q → 0) tilts the barycenter toward the semantic space of the relationship type itself.
The BaryEdge is also enriched with a registry.summary — a short natural-language description of what the connection means, generated by an LLM at ingestion time. This summary is embedded independently and contributes to the BaryEdge's retrievability. When a query lands near the barycenter, it implicitly surfaces both parent CMs.
2.4  MetaBary: Baryedges as CMs
At level 8 and above, BaryEdges become eligible to act as CMs for new connections. Two BaryEdges that are themselves related can be connected by a MetaBary:
BaryEdge_A  →  MetaBary  →  BaryEdge_B
MetaBary connections encode cross-paradigm insight: the relationship between relationships. A MetaBary between "flyby anomaly ↔ Pioneer anomaly" and "dark matter rotation curves ↔ MOND" encodes the observation that both pairs describe unexplained gravitational excess at system boundaries — a connection no individual paper cluster points toward.
Validity constraint: a MetaBary is only created when its two BaryEdge-CMs share at least one common ancestor node in their CM1/CM2 lineage. This prevents spurious connections between genuinely unrelated structures and ensures MetaBarys represent real hierarchy overlap.

4. Ingestion Pipeline
4.1  Level 14–15: Paper Nodes
Each paper is embedded (title + abstract) and stored as a CM Node at level 14. Named entities, specific claims, and measurements extracted from the paper become level-15 nodes linked upward.
4.2  BaryEdge Formation (Bottom-Up)
For each level from 15 up to 8, the pipeline runs:
    • Compute cosine similarity across all node pairs at this level.
    • For pairs above threshold (default 0.72), generate registry.summary via LLM call.
    • Compute bary_vec = normalize(q·v(cm1) + q·v(cm2) + (1−q)·v(edge_type)).
    • Embed registry.summary independently.
    • Store BaryEdge document with both vector fields.
    • Create HierarchyLinks from new BaryEdge upward.
The edge_type "same_phenomenon" is assigned when two nodes from different domains exceed the similarity threshold and their registry.summary contains cross-domain bridge language (detected by a classifier prompt).
4.3  Domain Nodes (Level 10–6)
Domain nodes are created by clustering BaryEdges from the level below. The domain node vector is the centroid of its children's vectors, weighted by q. This means a domain vector encodes not just its papers but all the relationship structure within it.
4.4  MetaBary Formation (Level 8 and Above)
Once BaryEdges exist at level 8, the pipeline checks for pairs of BaryEdges that share a common ancestor (via HierarchyLink traversal). Qualifying pairs with cosine similarity above a higher threshold (default 0.80) become MetaBary connections with is_metabary = true.

5. Query Patterns
5.1  Standard Retrieval (All Levels)
MongoDB Atlas Vector Search indexes the vector field across all doc_types simultaneously. A query returns a ranked mix of CM Nodes, BaryEdges, and MetaBarys. Each BaryEdge result implies its two parent CMs. Effective retrieved context is significantly larger than the raw top-k count.
5.2  Cross-Domain Bridge Query
The key novel query: given a concept in domain A, find BaryEdges of type "same_phenomenon" whose CM lineage crosses into domain B. This surfaces cross-domain anomaly clusters invisible to standard similarity search.

5.3  Hierarchy Traversal
Any retrieved BaryEdge can be traversed upward via its HierarchyLinks to find its parent domain node, and further to paradigm level. This answers: "what broader phenomenon connects these two papers?" without requiring the query to know the answer in advance.

6. Proof-of-Concept Candidates
6.1  Vessel Tracking (Recommended First)
The existing maritime vessel tracking system (MongoDB, ~800 vessels) is the ideal initial testbed. Vessels and ports are natural CM Nodes. Port calls are BaryEdges. Routes are HierarchyLinks. Ground truth (actual historical routes, delays, anomalies) is owned entirely, with no external validation dependency.
Success metric: does BaryGraph retrieval predict anomalous port behavior (unexpected delay, route deviation) better than flat vessel-history lookup? Measurable with existing data.
6.2  Legal Case Law
Each case cites precedents, has jurisdiction, has outcome. Two cases reaching opposite conclusions from the same precedent produce a BaryEdge with high divergence in registry.summary. Ground truth: does the system recover the precedent chain a practicing lawyer identifies as relevant? Binary pass/fail per query.
6.3  arXiv Physics Subdomains
Recommended scope: astro-ph.EP (planetary), astro-ph.SR (solar), gr-qc (gravity), hep-ph (particles) — approximately 80,000 papers. Key test: do "same_phenomenon" BaryEdges recover known cross-domain connections (e.g., flyby anomaly and dark matter, pulsar timing and gravitational wave background) before a domain expert is asked to confirm them?
6.4  Stack Overflow / GitHub Issues
Code problem as CM1, solution as CM2, BaryEdge = specific error pattern. Higher levels: language → framework → problem class. Measurable: does BaryGraph retrieval resolve a novel bug faster than standard search? Automated evaluation using resolved-issue ground truth.

7. Infrastructure
7.1  MongoDB Atlas
Single collection for all document types. Atlas Vector Search index on the vector field covers CM Nodes, BaryEdges, and MetaBarys simultaneously. Standard Atlas queries handle doc_type filtering, level range filtering, and HierarchyLink traversal. No separate vector database required.
7.2  Embedding Model
Any 1536-dim model (OpenAI text-embedding-3-small) is sufficient for the proof-of-concept. The BaryEdge vector computation is model-agnostic — it operates on whatever embedding space the CM Nodes use.

8. Key Architectural Rules
    • BaryEdges only connect nodes at the same hierarchy level. Cross-level connections are HierarchyLinks only.
    • A BaryEdge becomes eligible as a CM (for MetaBary connections) only at level 8 and above.
    • MetaBary formation requires a verified common ancestor — no spurious cross-paradigm connections.
    • The BaryEdge vector is always computed from its CM vectors, never stored independently of them. If a CM vector is updated, its BaryEdge vectors must be recomputed.
    • Connection quality q decays with staleness: q(t) = q₀ · exp(−λ·Δt). Stale BaryEdges are candidates for pruning or refresh.
    • Hierarchy traversal is strictly one level at a time. No direct jump from level 15 to level 3.
    • "same_phenomenon" edge type is the primary signal for cross-domain discovery. It is assigned by a classifier, not by cosine similarity alone.

9. Open Questions
    • Optimal similarity threshold for BaryEdge formation at each level — too low creates noise, too high misses real bridges.
    • Optimal q decay rate λ by domain — scientific papers decay slowly, news items decay fast.
    • Whether registry.summary embedding should be averaged into the BaryEdge vector or kept as a separate searchable field.
    • Whether the hierarchy level assignment for domain nodes should be fixed (Table 1) or learned from corpus structure.
    • Formal evaluation metric for cross-domain retrieval quality: precision and recall require a ground-truth set of known cross-domain connections.
    • Whether the MetaBary validity constraint (common ancestor) can be relaxed at levels 3–5 where paradigm-level connections are intentionally broad.
